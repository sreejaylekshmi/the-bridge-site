Your marketing data is probably lying to you (and how to tell)

Most businesses I look at don't have a marketing problem. They have a measurement problem wearing a marketing problem's clothes.

Before any analysis, the data itself has to be trustworthy, and in most small B2B companies it quietly isn't. Here are the five failures I check first, because any one of them makes every downstream number wrong.

1. Conversions that were never set up

GA4 tracks page views out of the box, but not form submissions, phone clicks or demo bookings unless someone configures them. If nobody did, your "conversion rate" is a number about nothing.

2. Untagged links

Share a link on LinkedIn without UTM parameters and the click arrives in your analytics with no origin. Enough of these and "Direct traffic" becomes a black hole hiding your best channel.

3. The dark social gap

Links forwarded in WhatsApp, Slack and email all show up as Direct too. B2B buying conversations happen in exactly these places, so the channel doing the real work often gets zero credit.

4. Your own team in the numbers

If internal traffic isn't filtered, ten employees checking the website daily can outnumber your genuine visitors, flattening every trend you think you're seeing.

5. Last-click thinking

A buyer reads three articles, attends a webinar, then Googles your name and converts. Last-click attribution hands all the credit to "brand search", and quietly argues for cutting the content that created the demand.

None of these show up as errors. Everything looks fine, but the numbers are just wrong. Which is why the first step of any engagement I do is an audit of the measurement itself, before believing a single chart.
